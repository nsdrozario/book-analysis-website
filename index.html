Symphony for the City of the Dead

M.T Anderson

Discover how, of all things, Dmitri Shostakovich's music saved his hometown from the two biggest dictators in modern history.

Tell me more! Let's take a listen!

Not just any biography

Symphony for the City of the Dead reads much like a narrative novel, enticing you into the exciting story of the complex web of events during World War 2, the rise of the USSR, and the Siege of Leningrad.

Something for everyone

Whether you're a classical music connoisseur, a history buff, or just someone who is fascinated by the seemingly outlandish proposition that a piece of music can change so much, you'll find something that grabs your interest.

A new look

While there is a breadth of literature on World War II and the USSR, chances are, you wouldn't expect to read it from the the point of view of a civilian, much less one of the most celebrated classical composers.

Impeccable writing

Anderson cleverly sprinkles things that give deeper insight into his ideas.

Tone

Ironic/Critical

"They met at Molotov’s office. The German ambassador recently had provided assurances that nothing was wrong. Now the ambassador announced that Germany and the USSR were officially in a state of war. Comrade Molotov, representative of one of the most brutal regimes in history, could do nothing but stare at him, shocked, and then whine, 'But what have we done to deserve this?'" (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead: Dmitri Shostakovich and the Siege of Leningrad", 162).

The sharp turn of events—the USSR finding itself at war with Germany—is made even more impactful by Anderson's intentional focus on how the USSR felt victimized whilst they victimized their own people.

Anderson's satiric exemplification of the atrocities the USSR committed not only suggests Anderson has a critical and disgusted attitude, but also evokes a yearning for justice from the reader, reminding them of the suffering that the Russian people have endured.

Confusion

"Shostakovich left the building. For the moment, he was free. As in some absurdist fable, his executioner was in line for execution. He wandered home across the Neva River, befogged and bewildered by his tenuous reprieve." (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 122)

Anderson conveys his astonishment by calling this brush with death an "absurdist fable", exaggerating how unbelievable Shostakovich's luck is, suggesting it is mythical. Anderson builds on this by describing the experience shown with polysyllabic diction, which results in the audience feel just as equally perplexed as Shostakovich and Anderson, by mirroring the complex web of events that resulted in Shostakovich's escape and the text's complexity.

Devices and Modes of Arrangement

Anderson's masterful stylistic choices enhance the book and will, without a doubt, keep you hooked.

Juxtaposition

“Easing their marriage even more … word came through that the couple could move into a vacated apartment in a building reserved for Soviet composers. Shostakovich and his pregnant physicist were delighted to move out of his childhood home. … This is how things stood when … Joseph Stalin decided it was time to see what the celebrated Dmitri Shostakovich was all about” (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 89).

Throughout the book, Anderson establishes Stalin as a symbol of death by using numerous examples of his purge of dissenters. But here, there is a tranquil image of a peaceful family life being placed right next to it. Anderson does this to foreshadow misfortune for the Shostakovich family. It brews tension and makes the reader feel fearful and anxious.

Wouldn't you want to find out what happens next? Anderson cleverly juxtaposed these scenes to keep the reader immersed into the story, tapping into the reader's curiosity and fear of of the clutches of evil grabbing Shostakovich.

Metonymy

“The story should end there, but of course it does not. History does not allow for perfect cadences” (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 344).

A perfect cadence in music is a series of harmonies that strongly resolves tension, much like how you would expect Shostakovich's story to have a clear ending. Anderson links the shared idea of resolution by using the term perfect cadence as a metonym to describe how Shostakovich's life was indeed still not free from the conflicts of his time. In any case, even if the reader does not see the metonym, the reader would feel a foreboding mood—Anderson makes it clear nonetheless that Shostakovich's life isn't free of troubles just yet, and only emphasizes this with a metonym.

Exemplification

"How were the Russians able to withstand this onslaught? It is one of the sick ironies of the war that they probably would not have been able to if they had not learned to absorb loss in the nightmare of Stalin’s purges. ... [Stalin] could plant NKVD gunners behind his soldiers and tell them to shoot if anyone showed signs of cowardice. He could send battalions of prisoners into battle, marching toward almost certain death. He could relocate millions of factory workers by command in the space of a few weeks. He could rely on the slave labor of millions in the gulag. As Sir Alan Brooke has written, 'It was the Russians who provided the oceans of blood necessary to defeat Germany'" (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 353).

Anderson's claim within this passage is that Stalin's regime caused so much suffering that it prepared the Russian people for their fight with the Nazis. Furthermore, it claims that Stalin's extreme policies helped him win the war, despite the unthinkable amount of lives lost in the process. By specifically showing examples of Stalin's policies, the reader is convinced that Anderson's assertion is correct—since the Russian people had grown used to the horrible conditions of Stalin's regime, they were prepared to do unthinkable things, because to them, they were living a life where such horrifying demands were a fact of life.

But should I read it?

Of course!

Anderson's clever word choice and meticulous pacing prevents the novel from ever becoming a drag. He balances action, suspense and exposition in such a way that you're always looking for what's next.

No matter what you're looking to get out of this novel, whether it be entertainment or knowledge, Anderson's novel provides both, with drama and nonfiction in equal measure.

Structuring this biography as a narrative does wonders for the reader's enjoyment—and the fact that the events of the book truly happened makes it all the more enjoyable.

While Anderson occasionally makes the somewhat obscure allusion, this is never to the detriment of your reading experience. Anderson never relies on devices for explaining his ideas—rather, he uses them to emphasize his ideas. Either way, Anderson makes sure that his ideas are clearly conveyed.

About the Author

Image of M. T. Anderson Source: https://mt-anderson.com/blog/wp-content/themes/titan/images/sidebar/sidebox.jpg

Why did Matthew Tobin Anderson write Symphony for the City of the Dead?

Let's start by looking at just who M. T. Anderson is.

  • Anderson usually writes young adult books (Anderson, "About").
    • According to him, he writes more mature themes for a younger audience because unlike older audiences, they hope for a better world (Anderson, "A Note from Author M. T. Anderson").
  • He was a classical music reviewer once (Anderson, "About").
    • He engaged with classical music before, even as a child. He actually first found Shostakovich's music as an older teenager (Anderson, "A Note from Author M. T. Anderson").

Why was Shostakovich so special to him?

Anderson found a great deal of depth in Shostakovich's music—his music had intricate layers of complexity that fascinated Anderson (Anderson, "A Note from Author M. T. Anderson").
This was quite like another situation in Anderson's childhood—he read Hiroshima by John Hersey, which gave him great insight into the moral complexity of war ("A Note from Author M. T. Anderson").

Recall Anderson's familiarity with classical music—this was key in him having deeper insight into Shostakovich's music. Without this, he wouldn't have taken enough interest to write about Shostakovich's life within the larger historical context of World War II and the rise of communism. Undoubtedly, this inspired Anderson to share his findings and his new perspective on this rather familiar topic.

Anderson conveys this quite nicely within the book itself:

"History is not simply the great tumults and tragedies but the accumulation of tiny moments and gestures" (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 367).

What does Anderson aim to accomplish with this book?

Anderson was driven by the bizarre and compelling chain of events that established how important Shostakovich's music was in the context of the rise of communism and World War II ("A Note from Author M. T. Anderson").

The novel perspective of World War II and communism that can be seen only through looking at Shostakovich's impact on this period inspired Anderson to write Symphony for the City of the Dead to both honor Shostakovich's legacy and to portray the fascinating history that enthralled him. What's more is, even though he writes for all sorts of people, he had one specific audience in mind as well; and it is deeply linked with what Anderson aims for his book to do.

A specific audience

Anderson wrote with young adults in mind. Why?

Anderson did so as a reflection of his own experiences. He knew that this story would be better suited for an audience ready for more mature topics, while young enough to still have aspirations and hopes of a better future, and still feel shock at injustice, much like his experience reading Hiroshima, and even listening to Shostakovich's music.

This is Anderson's way of making the world a better place: by informing the youth so that they are aware of historical injustices and cruelties so that they can be avoided in the future.

What's it really about?

As a nonfiction book, clearly one of the purposes is to inform. Obviously, one of the messages is the real events that transpired within the book—the actual description of the horrors people experienced during the Siege of Leningrad and the rise of the USSR.

However, we already know that Anderson has a deeper purpose—and a deeper message than what meets the eye.

Human emotion is the most impactful of all motivations.

Despite all of the suffering people endured during the Siege of Leningrad, things like music, which are sometimes regarded as frivolous—actually helped develop the determination to overcome the famine and the attacks by the Nazis.

"...the first to die among those in normally good condition were people of weak character, those who gave in morally, who lost the will to work and thought too much about their stomachs" (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 296).
"But for the people of Leningrad, [Shostakovich's 7th Symphony] meant something else entirely: it gave them an identity" (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 340).


The smallest of actions can make all the difference.

Yet another interpretation of the same ideas Anderson presents.

You'll discover that Shostakovich's 7th was key in mustering popular support in America for foreign aid to the USSR, which resulted in the tide turning against the Nazis on the battlegrounds.

"It would remind the West that Russians were not Bolshevik barbarians. They were writing symphonies even in the midst of siege" (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 302).

A key motif in Anderson's novel is how common people work together in small ways to face the overwhelming challenges of life during the Siege of Leningrad. Whether it was civilian volunteering for production or infrastructure, or performing and writing music, all of it was essential to stop the starvation and the senseless murder.

Given how Anderson aims to encourage positive change and discourage injustice, especially from one of his target audiences, young adults, it makes sense to suggest this message, because if Anderson appeals to an audience who isn't jaded over injustice yet, he must encourage them to believe in their power to make a difference.

Context

Symphony for the City of the Dead: Dmitri Shostakovich and the Siege of Leningrad was published in 2015, far after the events described in the book. However, these historical events were so impactful to all of history that they certainly did play a part in shaping this book.

Let's look at some of them.

Communism

  • Even today, communism's feasibility is debated by many.
    • Anderson does not make a specific argument to this end, but still condemns the atrocities committed by each of the dictators in the USSR's era.
    • Writing about the intricacies of economic systems wouldn't make sense for this book anyway. Anderson's motivation for writing the book was to explore Shostakovich's impact on the period, and as an effect his book contextualizes this period of time.
    • Furthermore, for Anderson's purpose, critiquing an economic system is irrelevant; however, he critiques the atrocities committed in the time period of the book—no matter who is responsible. This matches with the book's purpose of shaping the world such that these atrocities never happen again. However, he does not censor the relationship between the political ideologies of that time and the atrocities that happened.

World War II

World War II is a very familiar time period in history—it was still within modern history, and it's impact was so profound that it is one of the most deeply studied events in history.

However, most people study the political and military aspects of World War II. Who would have thought that classical music would have to do with any of this? Anderson notes difficulty in researching Shostakovich's life (Anderson, "Symphony for the City of the Dead", 368). However, if his motivation for writing the book was strong enough to look for the facts even with relatively few leads, the thrill of exploring a new aspect of a familiar time period would certainly be something that would further motivate Anderson and is why Anderson's novel focuses on both Shostakovich's music and it's impact.
