Irony

"They met at Molotov’s office. The German ambassador recently had provided assurances that nothing was wrong. Now the ambassador announced that Germany and the USSR were officially in a state of war. Comrade Molotov, representative of one of the most brutal regimes in history, could do nothing but stare at him, shocked, and then whine, 'But what have we done to deserve this?'" (Anderson 162)

The sharp turn of events—the USSR finding itself at war with Germany—is made even more impactful by Anderson's intentional focus on how the USSR felt victimized whilst they victimized their own people.

Anderson's attitude is clear -- his satiric exemplification of the atrocities the USSR committed evokes a yearning for justice from the reader, reminding them of the suffering that the Russian people have endured.

Devices and Modes of Arrangement

Anderson's masterful stylistic choices enhance the book and will, without a doubt, keep you hooked.

Juxtaposition

“Easing their marriage even more … word came through that the couple could move into a vacated apartment in a building reserved for Soviet composers. Shostakovich and his pregnant physicist were delighted to move out of his childhood home. … This is how things stood when … Joseph Stalin decided it was time to see what the celebrated Dmitri Shostakovich was all about” (Anderson 89).

Throughout the book, Anderson establishes Stalin as a symbol of death by using numerous examples of his purge of dissenters. But here, there is a tranquil image of a peaceful family life being placed right next to it. Anderson does this to foreshadow misfortune for the Shostakovich family. It brews tension and makes the reader feel fearful and anxious.

Wouldn't you want to find out what happens next? Anderson cleverly juxtaposed these scenes to keep the reader immersed into the story, tapping into the reader's curiosity and fear of Shostakovich, the protagoanist, from falling into the clutches of evil.

Why did Matthew Tobin Anderson write Symphony for the City of the Dead?

Let's start by looking at just who M. T. Anderson is.

  • Anderson usually writes young adult books.
  • He was a classical music reviewer once.
    • He engaged with classical music before, even as a child. He actually first found Shostakovich's music as an older teenager.

Why was Shostakovich so special to him?

Anderson found a great deal of depth in Shostakovich's music -- his music had intricate layers of complexity that fascinated Anderson. This was quite like another situation in Anderson's childhood -- he read Hiroshima by John Hersey, which gave him great insight into the moral complexity of war.

What does Anderson aim to accomplish with this book?

Anderson was driven by the bizarre and compelling chain of events that established how important Shostakovich's music was in the context of the rise of communism and World War II.

However, seeing how rare discourse was about the state of Russia during World War II, he sought to write Symphony for the City of the Dead to both honor Shostakovich's legacy and to portray the fascinating history that enthralled him. What's more is, even though he writes for all sorts of people, he had one specific audience in mind as well...

A specific audience

Anderson wrote with young adults in mind. Why?

Anderson did so as a reflection of his own experiences. He knew that this story would be better suited for an audience ready for more mature topics, while young enough to still have aspirations and hopes of a better future, and still feel shock at injustice, much like his expereince reading Hiroshima, and even listening to Shostakovich's music.

This is Anderson's way of making the world a better place: by informing the youth so that they are aware of historical injustices and cruelties so that they can be avoided in the future.

Context

Symphony for the City of the Dead: Dmitri Shostakovich and the Siege of Leningrad was published in 2015, far after the events described in the book. However, these historical events were so impactful to all of history that they certainly did play a part in shaping this book.

Let's look at some of them.

Communism

  • Even today, communism's feasibility is debated by many.
    • Anderson does not make a specific argument to this end, but still condemns the atrocities commited by each of the dictators in the USSR's era.
    • Anderson does not aim to make the book's message about economic systems; the book is written primarily from the perspective of the common people and their struggles, rather than debating economics.
    • Writing about the intricacies of economic systems wouldn't make sense for this book anyway. Anderson wrote this book not because of communism itself, but because of how it impacted Shostakovich (and by extension, the common person in Russia at the time.)
