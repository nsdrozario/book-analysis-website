Irony

"They met at Molotov’s office. The German ambassador recently had provided assurances that nothing was wrong. Now the ambassador announced that Germany and the USSR were officially in a state of war. Comrade Molotov, representative of one of the most brutal regimes in history, could do nothing but stare at him, shocked, and then whine, 'But what have we done to deserve this?'" (Anderson 162)
The sharp turn of events—the USSR finding itself at war with Germany—is made even more impactful by Anderson's intentional focus on how the USSR felt victimized whilst they victimized their own people. Quite an emotionally charged moment in the book, and forms the transition from peacetime to war in the book well.

Devices and Modes of Arrangement

Anderson's masterful stylistic choices enhance the book and will without a doubt keep you hooked.

Juxtaposition

“Easing their marriage even more … word came through that the couple could move into a vacated apartment in a building reserved for Soviet composers. Shostakovich and his pregnant physicist were delighted to move out of his childhood home. … This is how things stood when … Joseph Stalin decided it was time to see what the celebrated Dmitri Shostakovich was all about” (Anderson 89).
Throughout the book, Anderson establishes Stalin as a symbol of death by using numerous examples of his purge of dissenters. But here, there is a tranquil image of a peaceful family life being placed right next to it. Anderson does this to foreshadow misfortune for the Shostakovich family. It brews tension and makes the reader feel fearful and anxious. Wouldn't you want to find out what happens next? Well, you can find out by reading the book!

About the Author

Image of M. T. Anderson Source: https://mt-anderson.com/blog/wp-content/themes/titan/images/sidebar/sidebox.jpg

Why did Matthew Tobin Anderson write Symphony for the City of the Dead?

Anderson was a classical music reviewer and has a deep passion for classical music. Anderson, discovering the profound, unprecedented historical impact of Shostakovich's music, much like the moving nature of Shostakovich's symphonies themselves, wanted to share this unknown and surprising depth behind the events of the formation of the USSR and the outbreak of World War II.
